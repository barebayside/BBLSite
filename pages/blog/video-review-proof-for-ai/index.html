Simple videos & review proof that feed AI search

3 June 2026 7 min read

When someone asks an AI assistant who to call for your kind of service, the answer comes from whatever readable evidence that AI can find about your business — reviews, descriptions, video text, third-party mentions. The businesses that get named have more of that evidence, spread across more places. This guide covers the two easiest ways to build it: short video and review proof placed where AI can actually read it.

1What the research actually says about video and AI

The best data we have on YouTube citations in AI comes from a March 2026 OtterlyAI study that analysed over 100 million AI citations across ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Google AI Mode, Perplexity, Copilot, and Gemini.

Two findings matter most for local businesses:

  • View count is almost irrelevant. Near-zero correlation between citation frequency and views, likes, or subscriber count. More than 40% of AI-cited videos had fewer than 1,000 views. You don't need to go viral — you need to be findable.
  • Not all AI tools behave the same. Perplexity cited YouTube content in 38.7% of cases where social media was involved; Google AI Overviews at 36.6%; ChatGPT at only 4.4%. Video helps most with Perplexity and Google's AI products — be realistic about ChatGPT.

Two format signals also stand out from the same study:

  • 94% of AI-cited videos were long-form. YouTube Shorts accounted for only 5.7% of citations.
  • Videos with timestamps earned multiple citations far more often — timestamps let AI pull a specific section rather than the whole video.

What the AI is actually reading isn't the video itself. It's the title, description, and timestamps. That's the insight to carry into everything below.

2The simplest video most local businesses can make

You don't need editing software, a ring light, or a script. For almost any local service business, the most useful starting point is a 60-to-90-second before-and-after walkthrough filmed on a phone:

  1. Show the problem at the start
  2. Walk through what you did
  3. Show the result at the end
  4. Say the job type, the area, and what the customer originally called you about

This format works because it directly answers a question someone might ask an AI: "what does this type of job actually involve, and how does it turn out?"

Title the video the way someone would search it:

  • "Blocked drain cleared after tree roots — [area/city]" beats "We fixed it fast! ✨ Another happy customer."

Write the description in two or three sentences: cover the job type, what caused the problem, what was done, and where.

Add timestamps if the video has more than one stage:

  • "0:00 — blocked drain, 0:42 — clearing the roots, 1:15 — result"

Two minutes of work, and it turns your clip into something AI can extract a specific moment from. Description length has a modest positive correlation with citation frequency in the data. A one-line description leaves the most useful part undone.

3Where review proof needs to live

Most businesses collect reviews on Google and some on Facebook. That's a reasonable start — but there's a gap: your own website.

AI systems that crawl the web can read your website directly. If your homepage has real review text on it — not a third-party widget loading via JavaScript, but actual copied text — those words are part of what AI can draw on. The area mentioned in a review, the job type a customer described, the specific outcome they praised: all of that adds detail an AI can reference.

The fix:

  • Take three to five of your most specific reviews — ones that mention the job type and location.
  • Paste the text directly onto a page of your website (static text, not a widget).
  • Use the reviewer's first name.

Your Google Business Profile matters here too. The review text customers leave is indexed and readable — and so are your replies. A reply that mentions the job type and area ("Thanks Sarah, glad we could sort the leaking tap in [your area]") adds another small piece of readable context.

AI tools treat review text as primary evidence — not just star ratings. Specific, detailed review text across multiple readable sources is easier for AI to cite confidently than a high star average with nothing else.

Spread review proof across at least two or three platforms. ChatGPT sources local data from Bing Places, Yelp, and third-party directories — not Google directly. If your reviews only exist on Google, you're invisible to ChatGPT users.

4Putting one clip in four places

Filming the clip is the hard part. Distribution costs almost no extra time.

PlatformWhat to do
YouTubeUpload with full title, description, and timestamps
Your websiteEmbed the YouTube video on a service or "recent jobs" page
Google Business ProfileAdd the clip to the photo/video section
Facebook or InstagramShare with the description repurposed as a caption

One clip. Four placements. Each adds text and context in a different index: YouTube's, Google's, your own site, a social platform.

Once a fortnight is enough for most businesses — the goal is a pool of evidence that grows over time, not a burst campaign. Keep videos public. Unlisted videos are harder for AI systems to discover.

5How this fits with your answers page and your mentions

Video and review proof are one part of a three-part pattern:

  • Answers page — direct, readable answers to your most common customer questions. AI systems love these because they're already in the format AI wants to quote.
  • Third-party mentions — references on other sites (directories, community pages, industry associations). This is how AI builds confidence your business is real and established.
  • Video and review proof — readable evidence of actual work, in your words and your customers' words, across multiple sources.

Together they build a consistent body of readable evidence. You don't need all three before you start. For most businesses with no video presence, the first clip is the fastest win.

6Common mistakes to avoid

  • No description. A video without a description is nearly invisible to AI. Three sentences takes three minutes and does most of the work.
  • Using Shorts as your main format. Shorts account for only 5.7% of AI citations. Use long-form — even 60 to 90 seconds counts.
  • Reviews only on one platform. If your reviews are only on Google and ChatGPT uses Bing Places and Yelp, you're invisible to ChatGPT users.
  • JavaScript review widgets on your site. Many widgets pull text after the page loads, so a web crawler may never read the review content. Use static pasted text.
  • No timestamps. Timestamps let AI cite a specific moment. Two minutes to add; measurably increases how citable the video is.

Key takeaways

  • View count doesn't determine whether AI cites your video — 40%+ of AI-cited videos had fewer than 1,000 views.
  • Perplexity and Google AI Overviews are the most likely to cite YouTube; ChatGPT rarely does (4.4%).
  • Description length and timestamps have the clearest link to citation frequency — write the description, add timestamps.
  • Review proof needs to exist as static readable text on your website and on your Google listing at minimum.
  • One clip, four placements — YouTube, website, Google Business Profile, social — multiplies reach with almost no extra effort.
  • Video and review proof work best alongside an answers page and third-party mentions.

Common questions

Should I put videos on YouTube even though I have no subscribers?

Yes. Subscribers have near-zero correlation with AI citation frequency. What matters is that the video is public, plainly titled, and described.

How long should my video be?

Sixty to ninety seconds is a practical floor. The research shows 94% of AI-cited YouTube content is long-form. A two-minute walkthrough is fine.

Will this help me show up in ChatGPT?

Not much through YouTube directly — ChatGPT cites YouTube only about 4% of the time. For ChatGPT visibility, focus on Bing Places, Yelp, and third-party directories. Video helps most with Perplexity and Google's AI products.

How many reviews do I need on my website?

Three to five specific, job-describing quotes are enough to start. Add more over time — don't wait for a big collection.

What if I can't film on site?

Ask permission; respect a no. Alternatives: film the exterior result, narrate over a photo sequence, or focus on an explanation. If filming isn't practical, put more effort into the written description and review text instead.
