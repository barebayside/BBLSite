Turning a review theme into ad & landing-page copy

3 June 2026 6 min read

You've spent hours writing ads that felt clever at the time but didn't get clicks. Meanwhile, your customers have already written the best copy you'll ever use — it's sitting in your Google reviews, waiting. Here's how to find it and put it to work.

1Why your own words are usually the wrong ones

When you write copy about your own business, you default to what sounds impressive: "Professional service." "Quality workmanship." "Experienced team." It reads like every other local business, because it is.

Customers don't think in those words. They think in the thing that actually made them happy — the cleaner who was done before they got home, the physio who explained things without talking down to them. That specificity is what stops someone mid-scroll and makes them click.

This is called voice of customer copy. Your reviews are a public, searchable record of exactly how your customers talk about you — you don't have to guess.

97% of consumers read reviews when evaluating a local business, and 74% prioritise reviews from the past three months. — BrightLocal Local Consumer Review Survey 2026

2How to read your reviews for patterns

Don't skim for the nicest ones. Read for what keeps coming up.

Step by step:

  1. Open Google Business Profile and read every review in one sitting.
  2. Write down any phrase that appears more than once — even with different wording. "Showed up when he said." "Actually turned up on time." "Arrived exactly when promised." Those are three versions of the same theme: punctuality.
  3. Repeat across Facebook reviews, industry directories, and positive direct messages.
  4. Look for the thing customers reach for when explaining why they'd recommend you. That's your headline.

Common themes — but yours will differ, so read first:

  • Responsiveness (fast reply, same-day arrival)
  • Communication (kept them in the loop, never had to chase)
  • How the job was left (tidy, no mess, done properly)

Most businesses have two or three themes that repeat. Don't assume — read the reviews.

3Turning a theme into a headline

Once you've spotted the pattern, use the customers' words directly — not a polished version.

If you see "he actually cleaned up after himself," "left the place spotless," and "no mess at all afterwards" across eight reviews, your headline options are:

VersionType
"We Leave the Place Spotless — Or We Come Back."Direct + guarantee
"We Turn Up When We Say We Will."Simple statement
"No Chasing Required — We'll Keep You in the Loop."Reassurance
"A Landscaper Who Actually Cleans Up After the Job."Contrast to bad past experience

None of these are clever. That's the point. They land because the reader has had the opposite experience and is scanning for a reason to believe you're different.

"Professional and Tidy Service" is marketing padding. Customers didn't say that — you did.

4Using real review quotes as proof on the page

Your landing page needs proof, and the most credible proof you have is a direct quote from someone who already paid you.

Most businesses either skip quotes entirely, or drop glowing five-star praise at the bottom as decoration. What converts better is matching the quote to the specific fear the customer has at that point on the page.

  • Headline about punctuality → quote from a customer who was worried about a no-show and wasn't let down
  • Headline about tidiness → quote from someone who'd had a bad experience elsewhere and was relieved

You're placing evidence at the exact moment the reader might hesitate.

Use the quote verbatim. Don't clean it up. "He was there bang on 8am like he said, didn't have to ring him at all" converts better than "Punctual and professional." The rough edges are what make it believable.

5The rules around using real quotes

Both Google Ads and Meta allow genuine customer testimonials in ads, provided they're truthful and reflect what was actually said.

What you must not do:

  • Fabricate a quote or attribute words to someone who didn't say them
  • Edit a quote in a way that changes its meaning — even slightly
  • Cherry-pick to the point of distortion (hiding negatives while claiming a perfect record)
  • Republish quotes containing unsubstantiated health or outcome claims without being prepared to substantiate them yourself

Misleading testimonials break consumer-protection law in every major market and violate platform ad policies. This applies regardless of business size. Check the rules in your jurisdiction.

Sources: Google Ads Policies · Meta Advertising Standards

The practical upshot: quote real customers, quote them accurately, don't make claims you can't back up.

6What to avoid

  • Cherry-picking against the pattern. If nine reviews praise communication but one flags a timing issue, don't build an ad around speed. The negative tells you what to reassure people about.
  • Editing quotes until they sound fake. The moment a quote sounds like the business wrote it, it's worthless as proof — and if you changed the meaning, it's a legal problem.
  • Using generic praise. "Highly recommend!" is not usable. "Would recommend to anyone who's had trouble getting a reliable person to actually show up" is. Only use quotes that tell a story in a sentence.
  • Ignoring negative patterns. If three different people mention the same friction — confusing invoice, hard to get a callback — that's an operations note, not a review problem. Fix it before your next ad campaign.
  • Treating one great review as a theme. One quote from your happiest customer isn't a theme. Six different customers mentioning the same thing unprompted — that's a theme worth building a headline around.

Key takeaways

  • Read all reviews for repetition, not for the best one. Themes across multiple customers belong in your ads.
  • Use customers' words directly — specific, plain language outperforms polished marketing copy every time.
  • Place quotes at the moment of hesitation on your landing page, not just at the bottom as decoration.
  • Quote accurately and never change the meaning — misleading testimonials carry legal penalties in every major market.
  • Recent reviews carry more weight: 74% of consumers prioritise the past three months (BrightLocal LCRS 2026) — keep building new ones, not just mining old ones.
  • Use negative patterns as an operations signal — fix the issue before the next round of ads.

Common questions

What if I don't have many reviews yet?

Work with what you have. Even four or five reviews will usually show one shared theme. If you have only one or two, prioritise getting more before spending heavily on ads — without proof, the copy can't do its job.

Can I use reviews from platforms other than Google?

Yes. Facebook reviews, directory quotes, even positive direct messages count — as long as they're in the customer's words and unprompted. Be clear about the source and never quote something you wrote yourself.

What if my reviews are mostly about price?

Sit with that. Reviews mentioning value (felt worth what they paid) are useful copy material. Reviews mentioning low price are a pricing conversation first.

How many reviews do I need before a pattern is reliable?

You can usually spot a theme from ten reviews. Twenty or more makes it solid enough to build ads around with confidence.

Can I tidy up a quote before using it?

Fix an obvious typo. Don't cut context that changes what was said, rephrase for clarity, or remove a qualification the customer included. Quote it as written or don't use it.
