Writing a suburb page that ranks - and doesn't sound fake

3 June 2026 6 min read

You rank well at home but disappear the moment someone in the next suburb searches. So you copy your homepage, swap the suburb name, publish ten of them in an afternoon — and nothing changes. In fact, your whole site gets quieter. Here's why that backfires and what a real suburb page looks like.

1The two different results on Google's local page

Google's local results have two separate sections. Suburb pages can only win one of them.

The map box (three listings with pins) is proximity-driven. Businesses physically close to the searcher get priority. A page you publish won't change that — Sterling Sky: "You cannot SEO your way next to the searcher."

The website results below the map box are relevance-driven. On-page signals account for roughly a third of local organic ranking — the highest share of any single category (Whitespark 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors). A well-written page about work you do in a neighbouring area can rank there for searches like "[service] [suburb]". That's the realistic prize.

2Why copy-paste suburb pages get punished

Google's spam policy explicitly names city and suburb pages as a textbook example of doorway pages — pages created to rank for specific searches that give visitors nothing genuinely useful.

Google's spam policies state that doorway pages and scaled content abuse can result in the site ranking lower or not appearing in results at all. — Google Search Central

The damage isn't limited to those pages. Google can downgrade trust across the whole site, pulling down your homepage too. Sites with a batch of identical suburb pages often perform worse overall than before they existed.

The safe pattern (Sterling Sky, BrightLocal): roughly 80% genuine service content, 20% specific to each suburb — real jobs, real photos, real local detail.

3How many suburb pages is the right number?

Quality is the limit, not a count. Practitioner consensus: a few dozen is workable for most service businesses. Hundreds of near-identical pages sharply increases the risk of scaled content abuse treatment.

Start with suburbs where you already do most of your work — check job records, invoices, customer messages. Those are your first pages.

Avoid: suburbs you've barely serviced, never worked in, or are targeting purely because they seem affluent.

Sterling Sky's case study: 35 suburb pages with 84% content match ranked and generated leads because they served real users. Intent is what Google's reviewers look at.

4What goes on a real suburb page

The test: could a local read it and feel it was written by someone who's actually worked there? If yes, you've got something. If it could have been written by anyone with a thesaurus and a suburb name, you don't.

Step 1: Lead with the job, not the suburb

Don't open with "If you're looking for [service] in [suburb], look no further."

Open with the kind of work you actually do there:

  • Weak: "We are your trusted [trade] in [suburb]."
  • Strong: "Most of the work we do in this area is on older block gardens — overgrown edges, blocked drainage, and tight side-access that rules out large machinery."

Step 2: Reference local detail only someone who's been there would know

Mention the types of properties common in that area and what makes jobs there different. This isn't keyword-stuffing — it's writing something that could only come from experience.

  • Generic: "We service all home types in [suburb]."
  • Specific: "Most jobs in this neighbourhood are in 1970s brick homes — original plumbing runs under slab and the hot water systems are long overdue."

Step 3: Document a real job

If you've done work there, describe what it involved — the service, the problem, the outcome, anything specific to the property. You don't need the customer's name. A short, honest description of a real job does more than three paragraphs of generic copy. If you haven't done a job there yet, talk about common property types and requests in the area. Don't invent a job that didn't happen.

Step 4: Answer one question specific to that suburb

What do people in that area commonly ask? One paragraph on a genuinely local question gives the page a reason to exist beyond ranking:

  • Older suburb? Heritage guidelines or period-appropriate materials.
  • New estate? Stormwater management or homeowners' association approvals.

Step 5: Add a clear next step

Make it obvious what to do: call, email, or fill in a form. Include your phone number on the page — not just in the header. A simple "We quote [suburb] and surrounds — call us for a free assessment" is enough.

5Common mistakes to avoid

  • Targeting suburbs you don't actually service. Pages for areas you'd theoretically travel to mislead readers and waste effort.
  • Adding suburbs to your GBP service area as a ranking tactic. The service area field tells Google where you work — it does not cause you to rank there. Sterling Sky tested this directly. Display-only.
  • Publishing a bulk batch all at once. A handful of genuine pages over a few weeks looks natural. Twenty on the same day looks like a content farm.
  • Skipping internal links. Each suburb page should link to your main service pages and ideally one or two related suburb pages.
  • Treating the page as a one-off. Pages updated over time with new job details outperform static ones.

6Reviews are the paired lever

Suburb pages win the organic results. Fresh reviews lift the map box. Both work together.

Review signals account for roughly 20% of local map pack ranking — up from ~16% in 2023, with recency and sentiment carrying more weight than raw count (BrightLocal). A suburb page without a steady flow of recent reviews leaves half the work undone.

Key takeaways

  • Suburb pages win website results, not the map box. Proximity drives the map box; relevance drives organic results below it.
  • Google explicitly calls near-identical suburb pages doorway pages and acts against them — sometimes across the whole site.
  • A few dozen genuine pages beats hundreds of thin ones. Quality is the limit, not a count.
  • 80/20 rule: ~80% solid service content, ~20% suburb-specific — real jobs, real detail, real photos.
  • Reviews are the paired lever. Recent reviews lift map box visibility; suburb pages lift organic rankings. You need both.
  • Adding suburbs to your GBP service area is not a ranking tactic. Confirmed by direct testing — display only (Sterling Sky).

Common questions

Should I put every suburb I service on my homepage, or make separate pages?

List your service area briefly on the homepage. A homepage crammed with suburb names reads badly and doesn't rank for any of them. The detail belongs on individual pages.

How long should a suburb page be?

400–600 words is usually enough. One job story, one piece of local detail, one answered question, and a clear CTA. Don't pad it.

Will a suburb page help me show up in the map box?

Probably not. The map box is driven by physical location. A suburb page helps you rank in the website results below the map — worth having, especially for longer, more specific searches.

How do I know which suburbs to write pages for first?

Look at where your paying customers come from. Job records and enquiry history are the most reliable guide. Start with suburbs already sending you work but where you don't yet have a page.

Can I target a suburb I haven't done much work in yet?

Yes, but write honestly. Talk about property types and common requests rather than inventing jobs. As you do work there, update the page with real specifics.
