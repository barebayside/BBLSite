Keeping your site fast & your details consistent

3 June 2026 5 min read

Your site could be doing everything else right — good photos, solid reviews, the right suburbs listed — and still quietly lose rankings because it's slow or your business name appears differently across the web. Neither problem feels urgent. Both matter more than most owners realise.

1Why a slow site hurts you twice

Visitors aren't patient. If your site takes more than a couple of seconds to load, a large share leave before reading a word — back to search results, clicking the next option. That's the bookings problem.

The ranking problem is separate. Google measures how people use your site. Visitors who bounce immediately signal your page didn't deliver — and your ranking quietly slides. A slow site costs you bookings directly and damages your position in results at the same time.

Google has confirmed that page speed is a ranking factor for mobile search. — Google Search Central

2What Google actually measures (plain English)

Google scores site performance using three measurements called Core Web Vitals. Here's what they mean:

LCP — how fast the main content loads. Largest Contentful Paint. Time for the biggest visible element (usually a photo or heading) to appear. Under 2.5 seconds = good. Above 4 seconds = poor.

INP — how quickly the page responds when someone taps or clicks. Interaction to Next Paint. This replaced the older FID metric in March 2024 — any article still citing FID as current is out of date. INP measures every tap and click throughout the visit. Under 200 milliseconds = good. Above 500 milliseconds = poor.

CLS — whether the page jumps around while loading. Cumulative Layout Shift. Measures how much the layout moves as it loads. Under 0.1 = good. Above 0.25 = poor.

Currently only around half of mobile pages pass all three — if yours does, you're already ahead of most competitors. Slow sites with poor scores are also reportedly less likely to appear in Google's AI Overviews, an increasingly important traffic source.

3What makes a small-business site slow

Most slow small-business sites share the same four culprits:

  • Unresized images. Photos uploaded straight from a phone are often several megabytes each. Your site loads them at full size every visit. One or two of these and your site is already dragging — this is the most common problem by far.
  • Too many plugins. Every plugin adds something to load. Review widgets, chat tools, popup plugins — they all add weight.
  • Cheap shared hosting. Budget plans put many websites on the same server. When others are busy, yours slows down.
  • Caching switched off. Caching saves a ready-to-serve version of each page so it doesn't rebuild from scratch on every visit. If it's off, your site does unnecessary work every time someone loads it.

4Quick wins you can do this week

No rebuild needed. These are high-return fixes most owners handle without technical help:

  • Resize images before uploading. Use Squoosh (squoosh.app) or TinyPNG (tinypng.com) — both free. Aim for under 200KB per image, under 100KB for thumbnails. This single fix makes the biggest speed difference on most small-business sites.
  • Run a free speed test. Go to pagespeed.web.dev. Start with anything flagged red and anything labelled "image size."
  • Deactivate unused plugins. Review what's installed and remove anything you haven't actively chosen to keep.
  • Review your hosting plan. If your site scores poorly and images aren't the issue, hosting may be the constraint. Look for plans with "SSD" and "LiteSpeed" (or equivalent).

5NAP consistency: what it is and why mismatches cause problems

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone — the three details that need to match everywhere your business appears online: your website, your Google Business Profile, and directories like Yellow Pages, Yelp, and Apple Maps.

Google uses these signals to verify your business is real and correctly located. Consistent NAP is a trust signal. Inconsistencies — different phone formats, slightly different business names, an old address on a forgotten directory — create ambiguity Google has to resolve.

Research by BrightLocal found that businesses with accurate NAP across at least 40 authoritative directories averaged 19% higher Google Maps visibility than those with fragmented listings. Accuracy across major platforms beats volume across hundreds of minor ones. Getting Google, Facebook, Apple Maps, Yelp, and Yellow Pages right is far more valuable than chasing obscure directories.

"Citations rarely become the reason you win — they become the reason you're trusted enough to be considered." — BrightLocal

The most common mismatch is drift, not fraud. A business changes its phone number, updates the website, forgets the directories. A name gets abbreviated differently on different platforms. None of it intentional. All of it counts.

6Your 10-minute self-audit

Run this every three to six months. It catches drift before it compounds.

  1. Check your Google Business Profile. Search your business name on Google Maps. Write down the name, address, and phone exactly as they appear — spelling and punctuation included. This is your source of truth.
  2. Check your website footer. Most sites show address and phone in the footer. Compare it character by character to your Google listing — same phone format? Same street abbreviation? Fix any difference.
  3. Search your business name on Google. Click through the top two or three directory listings and check the NAP on each. Most directories let you claim and correct your listing for free.
  4. Run PageSpeed Insights on your homepage. Go to pagespeed.web.dev, enter your URL. A mobile score below 50 is affecting you. Note the red items — those are your priorities.
  5. Spot-check your images. Open browser developer tools (press F12), go to the Network tab, filter by images. Large uncompressed photos loading = your first fix.

7Common mistakes to avoid

  • Different phone formats across platforms. Pick one and use it everywhere — the same number written differently can register as two separate numbers.
  • Old address still on directories after moving. Update every directory you can find, not just the main ones.
  • Ignoring listings you didn't create. Directories sometimes pull your details automatically and get them wrong. Search your name in quotes regularly.
  • Chasing hundreds of citations. The ranking benefit drops off quickly past the major platforms.
  • Only checking speed once. Speed changes when you add content, install plugins, or your host has capacity issues. Build PageSpeed into your quarterly routine.

Key takeaways

  • A slow site costs you twice — visitors leave before reading anything, and Google notices the behaviour, pulling your ranking down.
  • Google measures three things: LCP (main content load speed), INP (response to taps and clicks — replaced FID in March 2024), and CLS (page stability during load).
  • Unresized images are the most common culprit. Compress photos before uploading and replace oversized ones already on your site.
  • NAP = Name, Address, Phone. These three details must match across your website, Google listing, and major directories.
  • Accuracy beats volume for citations. The big directories done right matter far more than hundreds of smaller ones.
  • The 10-minute audit is repeatable. Run it every few months to catch drift before it compounds.

Common questions

How do I find all the directories my business is listed on?

Search your business name in quotes on Google. Moz Local's free listing checker (search "Moz Local check listing") gives a broader snapshot.

Does site speed matter as much on desktop as mobile?

Google primarily uses your mobile site for ranking. Check your mobile score in PageSpeed Insights first.

What if I can't fix everything PageSpeed Insights flags?

Focus on red items — image size and anything blocking page rendering. Yellow and green items are refinements, not urgent.

I'm on Wix or Squarespace — can I still improve speed?

Yes, within limits. These platforms handle hosting and caching. Your biggest lever is image size — resize before uploading and avoid embedding too many third-party widgets.

How quickly will things improve after I fix these?

Speed improvements can be picked up by Google within a few weeks once pages are recrawled. NAP consistency takes longer — directories update at their own pace, Google reweights them gradually. Think months, not days. The goal is removing quiet ranking drags, not chasing a quick spike.
