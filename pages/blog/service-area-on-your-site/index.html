Adding your service area to your site — the right way

3 June 2026 5 min read

You've listed every suburb in your Google Business Profile service-area field. You've waited. You're still not ranking there. Here's why — and what actually works.

1The myth, busted plainly

The GBP service-area field tells customers where you're willing to travel. That's its only job.

It does not improve your ranking. Sterling Sky tested this directly: their office is in one city, they listed a major nearby city as a service area, and they kept ranking based on their verified address — not the listed areas. Their conclusion, confirmed through 2025: "The only impact that the service area has is visual." Adding or removing suburbs has no measurable effect on where you appear in search results.

Keep the field accurate. Just don't treat it as an SEO lever. — Sterling Sky, confirmed 2025

2Two very different results on the same page

When someone searches "plumber [suburb]" or "dog groomer [suburb]," they see two distinct result types — and they work completely differently.

  • The map box (3 local listings + map) — driven heavily by physical proximity. If you're 40 minutes away, it's very hard to appear here for that suburb. Distance is a hard constraint — no settings change fixes it.
  • Organic links below the map — driven by relevance, not proximity. A business 30 minutes away can rank here with a genuine page. This is where distant suburbs are actually winnable.

Most businesses chase the map box in places they can't win, while ignoring the organic results where they could.

3Where to list your service area on your site

Your website needs to clearly state where you operate — for visitors and for Google. Four places that matter:

  • Homepage — mention your region near the top. "Serving [city] and surrounding areas" in the intro answers "do you come to me?" immediately.
  • Contact or about page — customers check here specifically to confirm coverage. Broad, honest regional language is enough. Listing areas you don't cover saves everyone's time.
  • Service pages — if you have individual pages per service, add geographic context to each. Google indexes these pages; the coverage signal helps.
  • A dedicated "Areas We Serve" page (optional) — useful for large operations covering a wide area. One short paragraph per region. Only list places you genuinely service.

4What actually gets you into suburb results

For organic results — the winnable ones — you need real suburb-specific pages. Not a footer list of suburb names. Not a page that repeats the suburb five times with no other content. A real page a customer from that area would actually read.

What that means in practice:

  • Covers the service itself
  • Mentions the suburb naturally, not stuffed
  • Includes local context where you genuinely have it
  • Gives someone from that area a reason to trust you

Start with two or three suburbs you already work in regularly. Write proper pages. Build from there. See Writing a Suburb Page That Ranks and Building Suburb Pages That Rank for the full how-to.

Focused effort on fewer, better pages beats a long list of shallow ones — every time (Whitespark, 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors; BrightLocal, Service Area Pages Guide).

5Setting realistic expectations

If your business is physically far from a suburb, you won't crack the map box there. That's proximity at work — not a problem to solve with settings.

The organic results are a different story. But be honest about which suburbs are worth targeting. If you travel 90 minutes for the occasional job somewhere, you probably don't need to rank there. Focus suburb pages on areas where you want consistent work.

6Common mistakes to avoid

  • Treating the GBP service-area field as a ranking tool. It isn't. Sterling Sky confirmed this with direct testing.
  • Listing suburbs you rarely visit. It doesn't help ranking and misleads customers.
  • Chasing the map box in distant suburbs. Put that effort into organic results through real suburb pages instead.
  • Writing shallow pages just to tick a box. One thin paragraph with a suburb name repeated doesn't rank. Google can tell.
  • Not linking to your suburb or service-area pages. Pages with no internal links are hard for Google to find.

Key takeaways

  • GBP service-area field = customer clarity, not ranking. Fill it in accurately; don't rely on it for search visibility.
  • Map box vs organic links work differently. Distance drives the map box; relevance drives organic results.
  • Organic results in distant areas are winnable — with genuine suburb pages, not GBP settings.
  • State your coverage clearly on your homepage, contact page, and service pages.
  • Depth beats breadth. Two or three strong suburb pages outperform twenty thin ones.

Common questions

Should I bother filling in the GBP service-area field?

Yes — for customer clarity. Fill it in accurately so visitors can see if you cover their area. Just don't expect it to rank you.

How many suburb pages do I need?

Start with two or three suburbs where you want regular work. Write them properly. Expand after a few months once you can see what's working.

Can I rank in suburbs far from where I'm based?

In the map box, it's very difficult — proximity is a hard constraint. In organic results below the map, yes, a genuine page can get you there.

Should I list every suburb I've ever worked in?

No. List areas where you want regular work. One-off jobs far away don't need to be in your service area.

Is there a quick fix for suburb ranking?

No. The closest thing is making sure your existing site clearly states where you operate — homepage, contact page, service pages. That gives Google the right context while you build proper suburb pages.
