LOCAL MARKETING

A simple after-job review-request system

3 June 2026 6 min read

You do great work, but you only ask for a review when you remember — so you've got a handful of them while the business down the road has three times as many. It's not that your customers wouldn't leave one. It's that nobody asked at the right moment, in a way that took ten seconds. The fix is a small system: ask on the spot while the customer is still happy, make it a 30-second job, and have an automatic backup for the ones you miss.

1Why a handful of reviews quietly costs you work

Reviews affect whether you show up in Google's local results — the map box with three businesses at the top.

Google lists review count and review score among the factors that influence local ranking. — Google Business Profile Help. At Bare Bayside Labs, this is the first thing we check when a business isn't showing up for nearby areas.

The trust side matters just as much:

  • A listing with 12 old reviews loses to one with 90 recent ones, even when the work is identical.
  • Most people read reviews before they ring a local business. — BrightLocal Local Consumer Review Survey
  • Businesses with steady, recent reviews get the call first.

2Why most owners ask late, or not at all

Two problems kill the ask:

  • Timing — You're busy doing the actual work. By the time the job wraps, the next one's already on your mind. The ask gets pushed to "later" — and later never comes. A week on, the customer's moved on, and a vague "could you leave us a review sometime?" gets a polite nod and nothing else.
  • Friction — If leaving a review means searching for your business, scrolling, and working out where to click, most people give up. Every extra step costs you a review.

3The system: ask on the spot, every job

Set this up once and it runs whether you remember or not.

Step 1: Ask the moment they're happy — in person

Ask when the customer can see the finished result and they're pleased. Don't wait, don't "follow up later."

Try this: "Really glad you're happy with it. The biggest thing you could do for a small business like mine is a quick Google review — have you got 30 seconds now?"

When you're standing in front of someone who just had a good experience, most say yes.

Step 2: Hand them the card and let them do it on the spot

  • Pull out a small review card with a QR code that opens your Google review page.
  • Let them scan and leave the review before you've packed up.
  • Keep cards in your wallet, vehicle, and folder — wherever you'll have them on hand.

No searching, no typing your business name, no "I'll get to it" — that's the difference between a review that happens and one that doesn't. A printed QR-code card costs almost nothing and is the single best review tool you can carry.

Step 3: Have an automatic backup for the ones you miss

Some jobs don't end face to face; some customers are in a rush. A same-day message with a direct link picks up the slack.

Get your direct link: Open Google Business Profile → "Ask for reviews" → copy the short link. It drops the customer straight onto the star rating.

Message that works:

"Thanks for your business today, [name]. If you were happy with how it went, a quick review would mean a lot — takes 30 seconds: [link]."

Better still, make it automatic. If you use any job-management or invoicing tool, turn on its review-request feature so it sends that message a few hours after a job is marked complete. Every job you didn't catch in person still gets the ask, without you lifting a finger.

4Reply to every review

Replying is the other half of the system — and most competitors skip it.

  • Reply within a day or two: thank them by name, mention the job.
  • Shows future customers you're switched on.
  • Signals to Google that the listing is active.

A quick "Thanks Sarah, glad we could sort that out for you before the weekend" does more than a generic "thanks for your feedback."

5Common mistakes to avoid

  • Waiting until later to ask. The review you get on the spot is worth ten you "follow up on."
  • Asking everyone at once. A sudden flood looks unnatural. Build the habit job by job so reviews come in steadily.
  • Buying or faking reviews. It breaks Google's rules, it's obvious, and it can get your listing penalised.
  • Only asking customers you're sure about. Ask every customer you did good work for — if you only chase guaranteed five-stars, your count stalls.

Key takeaways

  • Ask on the spot — the moment the customer is happy, before you pack up.
  • Carry a QR-code card so they can leave the review then and there.
  • Kill the friction — no searching, no typing, no "I'll do it later."
  • Automate a same-day backup for customers you don't catch face to face.
  • Reply to every review — builds trust with future customers and keeps your listing active.

Common questions

How many reviews should I be aiming for?

More than your nearest competitor, and steadily growing. A handful of new reviews every month beats a big batch once a year — recency counts, both to customers and to Google.

Is it okay to offer a discount for a review?

No. Paying for or incentivising reviews breaks Google's policies and risks your listing. Just ask — most happy customers are glad to help when you make it easy.

What if I get a bad review?

Reply calmly, take it offline, and fix what you can. One measured reply to a bad review often impresses future customers more than a wall of perfect five-stars.

What's the single most important step?

Asking on the spot with the card. Catching the customer while they're happy and making it a 30-second scan is the difference between "I'll do it later" and a review that's actually left.
