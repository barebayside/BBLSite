Phone video for tradespeople

3 June 2026 6 min read

You've seen the short clips local businesses put out — a job in progress, a before-and-after, someone walking through what they just did. Those aren't made with a camera crew. They're shot on the same phone in your pocket, on a site you're already on, in under five minutes. The difference: knowing how to hold the phone, which shots to take, and why captions — the step most people skip — determine whether the video gets watched.

1Why short video is worth the few minutes it takes

Short-form vertical video — Reels on Instagram and Facebook, TikTok, YouTube Shorts — is the format platforms push to people who don't follow you yet. Platforms have shifted from showing content to your existing followers to distributing it based on what people seem interested in (the interest graph). A small account can reach plenty of local people with a good clip.

  • According to Loopex Digital (2026), 55% of Instagram Reels views come from non-followers.
  • Instagram's Trial Reels feature shows new Reels to non-followers first to test performance before surfacing to your existing audience.

A decent 30-second clip can reach more local people than a well-written post, without paying for ads. Hootsuite and Sprout Social (2026) show authentic, real-person content outperforms heavily produced content — consumers rank human-generated content their top priority. It also builds trust faster.

2The two things to fix before you film

Most bad phone video isn't bad because of the filming — it's two 30-second fixes.

Hold the phone vertical. Reels, TikTok, and Shorts are built for 9:16 portrait format. Horizontal video posted to these platforms gets shrunk with black bars and looks wrong on a phone screen.

Face the light. Filming with a window or sun behind you turns your face into a silhouette. Face the light source — a window, the sun, or a $30–$50 LED ring light angled slightly above eye level.

Bonus: audio. Your phone mic works in quiet spots. On a noisy site, a clip-on lapel mic ($30–$50) makes more difference to perceived quality than video resolution does.

3The three shots that always work

You don't need a new idea every time. These three shots cover almost every job:

  • Shot 1 — Before: Ten seconds. Hold the phone up, show the problem. The overgrown lawn, the blocked drain, the cracked render. No narration needed.
  • Shot 2 — The work: A few seconds mid-job. Tools going, material applied. Enough to show it's happening — not the whole process.
  • Shot 3 — The result: Finished job at its best. Good light, camera still, angle that shows everything. Before-and-after is consistently cited as the highest-performing format for service businesses. Take five extra seconds to frame it well.

Three shots, one complete story — problem, process, solution — in under 30 seconds. No extra planning.

4Captions are not optional

Most people watch social video without sound. Research cited by Maestra (2026) puts this at over 85% of social video watched muted. Captioned video sees substantially higher completion rates — viewers who can follow along without audio are far more likely to watch to the end.

How to add captions — step by step

  1. Upload your video to the platform (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, or Facebook Reels).
  2. Turn on auto-captions — all four platforms offer this natively.
  3. Spend 30 seconds checking for errors. The AI is good but not perfect.
  4. Done.

For burned-in captions (travel with the video wherever it's shared), CapCut and OpusClip add them in a couple of taps. Keep text between 20%–70% from the top (platform controls sit at the bottom); white text on a dark semi-transparent background reads best.

Skipping captions is the most common thing local businesses skip — and it directly costs watch-through.

5Talking to camera

Nobody expects local business video to look like a TV ad. Keep it to one sentence:

  • "Blocked drain cleared in about an hour — good result for a Friday afternoon."
  • "Fresh coat on a tired timber-clad wall — makes a big difference."

Press stop. No sign-off needed.

If talking to camera makes you uncomfortable, don't. Film the before, the work, the result, and let auto-captions or a text overlay do the talking.

One tip: film in a quiet moment between jobs. Talk like you're explaining it to a customer next to you. Do one take. Don't watch it back immediately — most people talk themselves out of posting the first few times.

6A quick edit and how links work on these platforms

You don't need a laptop. Both iPhone (Photos or iMovie) and Android (Google Photos) let you trim and join clips in about 90 seconds:

  1. Import your before, work, and result clips.
  2. Trim dead air from each clip.
  3. Join in order.

Writing your caption

  • Describe what you did in plain language.
  • Add your area — local people who see their area mentioned stop scrolling.
  • Use 3–5 specific hashtags: your trade, your city, your service type. A wall of 30 doesn't help.

How links work (or don't)

  • Instagram and TikTok: links in captions don't work. Use "link in bio" — update your profile link and mention it in the caption.
  • Facebook: link posts in-feed get almost no organic reach. Put the link in the first comment, or use a Story.
  • YouTube: links in descriptions work fine.

7Common mistakes to avoid

  • Filming horizontal for Reels or TikTok. Vertical is the correct format.
  • Skipping captions. Over 85% of social video is watched muted (Maestra, 2026).
  • Filming into the light. Turn around — it takes three seconds and makes the biggest single difference.
  • Making it too long. 15–60 seconds is the sweet spot. Completion rate determines whether the algorithm keeps distributing your video — shorter has a structural advantage.
  • Waiting until the job looks perfect. The mid-work shot is often more compelling than the finished result.
  • Posting once and giving up. Six videos over six weeks builds more than five in one week then silence.

Key takeaways

  • Shoot vertical. 9:16 is the correct format for Reels, TikTok, and Shorts.
  • Face the light and use a clip-on mic on noisy sites — these do more than any editing.
  • Three shots cover everything: before, mid-job, result. Film them at every job.
  • Captions are required. Most video is watched muted. Turn on auto-captions when you upload.
  • One sentence to camera is enough. Talk like you're explaining it to a customer next to you.
  • Links don't work in Instagram or TikTok captions — use link in bio or a Story.
  • Small accounts still get reach. The interest graph means your video can reach non-followers regardless of follower count.

Common questions

Do I need to ask the customer's permission before filming?

If the job is inside their home, yes — ask before you start. Most say yes. For exterior work, a quick "mind if I film the result for our socials?" is good practice.

What if I'm not in the shot at all — does that matter?

No. The work is the subject, not you. Plenty of effective local business videos are just before-and-after with a caption.

My jobs aren't that visual. Does video still work?

More often than expected. Any hands-on service where something physically changes has a before-and-after story worth filming.

iPhone or Android — does it matter?

Both are more than good enough. Built-in camera app, no extras needed.

How often should I post video?

Once a week is solid. Once a fortnight is fine. Consistency beats volume — once a week for six weeks builds more momentum than five posts in one week then nothing for two months.
