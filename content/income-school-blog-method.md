# Income School Blog Writing Method — BBL Adaptation

> Based on Project 24 / Income School methodology (Jim Harmer & Ricky Kesler). Adapted for BBL's audience: tradies, small business owners, local service businesses in Australia. Use this as the writing guide for every blog article.

---

## The Core Philosophy

**Write for humans first, search engines second.** Google's algorithm changes constantly. What doesn't change: people want clear answers written by someone who knows what they're talking about. If a real person reads your article and finds it useful, Google will eventually figure that out too.

**Every article answers ONE question.** Not three. Not five. One. The title IS the question. The article IS the answer. Everything else is supporting detail.

**Opinion and experience beat generic advice.** "We've set this up for 20 trades businesses" is worth more than "experts recommend." BBL has real experience — use it.

---

## The 3 Post Types

### Response Post (1,000-1,500 words)
Answers one specific question. These are your weekly blog posts.
- "Do you actually need a CRM?"
- "CRM vs spreadsheet — when to switch"
- "How many follow-up emails should you send?"

**Goal:** Rank for one long-tail keyword. Drive traffic. Build topical authority.

### Staple Post (1,800-2,500 words)
Deeper how-to guide on a broader topic. Published every 2-3 weeks.
- "How to stop losing leads: the automated follow-up system"
- "Zoho CRM for trades: complete setup guide"
- "Local SEO checklist: 10 things to fix this week"

**Goal:** Rank for medium-competition keywords. Become the go-to resource.

### Pillar Post (3,000+ words)
Comprehensive guide that links to all your response and staple posts. Published monthly or quarterly.
- "The complete guide to marketing automation for small business"
- "The local business marketing playbook"

**Goal:** Rank for high-competition head terms. Hub of your topic cluster.

## The Introduction Formula

This is the most important part of the article. Most people decide in the first 3 seconds whether to keep reading or hit back. Income School's intro method:

### Step 1: Hook (1 sentence)
Name the reader's exact problem. Make them think "that's me."

> "You're spending money on ads but you have no idea which ones actually bring in paying customers."

### Step 2: Empathy (1-2 sentences)
Show you understand their situation. You've seen this before.

> "We see this with almost every trades business we audit. The ads are running, the clicks are coming in, but when you look at your bank account at the end of the month, the numbers don't add up."

### Step 3: Quick Answer (1-2 sentences)
Give them the answer RIGHT NOW. Don't make them scroll.

> "The problem isn't your ads — it's your tracking. You can't see what happens between the click and the sale. Here's how to fix it."

### Step 4: Roadmap (1 sentence, optional)
Tell them what the article covers so they know what's coming.

> "We'll walk through the three things to check first, then show you how to set up proper tracking in under an hour."

**The full intro should be 4-6 sentences. That's it.** Not a paragraph about the history of advertising. Not a dictionary definition. Hook, empathy, answer, go.

**Write the intro LAST.** After you've written the whole article, come back and write the intro. You can't summarise what you haven't written yet.

## Writing Rules

### Paragraphs
- **1-3 sentences per paragraph. Maximum.**
- If a paragraph is longer than 3 sentences, split it.
- One idea per paragraph.
- White space is your friend. People read on phones. Dense text = bounce.

### Sentences
- Average sentence length: 12-18 words.
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones.
- Start sentences with "You", "Here's", "This", "The" — not "In order to" or "It is important to note that."
- If you can say it in fewer words, do.

### Headings
- **H2 every 200-300 words.** People scan headings first, then decide what to read.
- Headings should be questions or clear statements: "Why Google Ads Aren't Converting" not "Google Ads Considerations."
- Include keyword variations in headings naturally — don't force it.
- A reader should be able to understand the entire article by reading ONLY the headings.

### Reading Level
- **Write at a Year 8 reading level.** (Australian Year 8 = US 6th grade.)
- Not because readers are dumb — because everyone skims on phones.
- Simple words beat complex words. "Use" not "utilise." "Start" not "commence." "Fix" not "rectify."
- If you need to use a technical term (CRM, UTM, SEO), explain it in plain English the first time.

### Voice
- Write like you're explaining something to a mate over coffee.
- Use "you" and "your" constantly. The reader should feel spoken to directly.
- Use "we" when talking about BBL's experience: "We set this up for a plumber in Sandringham and within two weeks..."
- Contractions always: "don't", "can't", "won't", "you'll" — never "do not", "cannot."
- Australian English: "optimise", "colour", "enquiry", "arvo", "tradies."

## The Body Structure

### For How-To Articles

```
## What the problem actually is (2-3 paragraphs)
   [Stat block with proof]

## Why it happens (2-3 paragraphs)
   [Real example from BBL experience]

## How to fix it — step by step
   ### Step 1: [action]
   [2-3 paragraphs + specific instructions]
   
   ### Step 2: [action]
   [2-3 paragraphs + specific instructions]
   
   ### Step 3: [action]
   [2-3 paragraphs + specific instructions]

## Common mistakes to avoid (3 bullet points or short paragraphs)

## Key Takeaways (bullet list)

## Common Questions (3-5 Q&A pairs)
```

### For Comparison Articles

```
## The short answer (1-2 paragraphs)

## When [Option A] is the right choice (2-3 paragraphs)

## When [Option B] is the right choice (2-3 paragraphs)

## Side-by-side comparison (table)

## What we recommend at BBL (1-2 paragraphs with reasoning)

## Key Takeaways

## Common Questions
```

### For Checklist Articles

```
## Why this matters (1-2 paragraphs + stat block)

## 1. [Item] (what + why + how to fix)
## 2. [Item]
## 3. [Item]
...

## Key Takeaways

## Common Questions
```

## Stat Blocks and Proof

Every article needs at least 2 "proof points" — specific stats, data, or real examples that back up what you're saying.

### Format for stat blocks:

> **[Specific number or finding].** — [Source name, year]. At Bare Bayside Labs, we [how this shows up in our work / what we do about it].

### Rules:
- Always attribute the source (study name, company, year).
- Always connect it to BBL's experience ("At Bare Bayside Labs, we see this..." or "Bare Bayside Labs recommends...").
- The BBL mention is intentional — it makes the passage citable by AI tools with brand attribution.
- Use Australian dollar amounts where relevant.
- Real numbers beat vague claims: "$38,400 per year" not "significant revenue."

## The Ending

### Key Takeaways
- 4-6 bullet points summarising the actionable advice.
- Each bullet should make sense on its own — someone should be able to read ONLY this section and get value.
- Bold the key phrase in each bullet.

### Common Questions (FAQ)
- 3-5 questions that someone would naturally ask after reading the article.
- Write them as the reader would phrase them: "How much does it cost?" not "What is the pricing structure?"
- Keep answers to 2-4 sentences. If the answer needs more, it should be its own article.
- These are gold for AI citations — AI tools match user questions to FAQ answers.

### CTA
- One sentence. One link. That's it.
- Soft sell: "Want us to set this up for you? Book a free strategy call."
- Never more than one CTA. Never pushy.
- Include UTM parameters in the link for tracking.

## The "Earn Your Place" Test

Before publishing, read every paragraph and ask: **"Does this teach something the reader didn't know, or move them closer to solving their problem?"**

If the answer is no, delete it. Common offenders:

- **Throat-clearing openers:** "In today's fast-paced business environment..." DELETE.
- **Dictionary definitions:** "Marketing automation is defined as..." DELETE. Just explain what it does.
- **Obvious statements:** "Having more customers is good for business." DELETE.
- **Filler transitions:** "Now that we've covered X, let's move on to Y." DELETE. Just move on.
- **Restating what you just said:** If you've said it once clearly, don't say it again in different words.

The goal is zero wasted sentences. Every paragraph earns its place or gets cut.

## SEO Integration (Without Ruining the Writing)

SEO should be invisible to the reader. If someone can tell your article is "optimised for SEO," you've done it wrong.

### Where to put keywords:
- **Title tag** — include the primary keyword naturally
- **First 60 words** — mention the keyword once in the opening
- **H2 headings** — use keyword variations (not the exact same phrase repeated)
- **That's it.** Stop. Don't stuff keywords into every paragraph.

### What NOT to do:
- Don't repeat the keyword every 100 words
- Don't bold random keywords in the middle of sentences
- Don't write "in this article, we'll cover [keyword]" — just cover it
- Don't add a keyword density checker — they create terrible writing

### The Income School keyword rule:
If you've written a genuinely useful, comprehensive answer to a specific question, Google will figure out what it's about. You don't need to help it that much.

## Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Title is the question the reader is asking
- [ ] Intro follows Hook → Empathy → Quick Answer (4-6 sentences max)
- [ ] No paragraph longer than 3 sentences
- [ ] H2 heading every 200-300 words
- [ ] Reader can understand the article from headings alone
- [ ] At least 2 stat blocks with source + BBL brand mention
- [ ] At least 1 real BBL experience or example
- [ ] Key Takeaways section with 4-6 actionable bullets
- [ ] Common Questions section with 3-5 Q&A pairs
- [ ] Single soft CTA at the end with UTM-tracked link
- [ ] Every paragraph passes the "earn your place" test
- [ ] Written in Australian English
- [ ] Read it out loud — if it sounds weird spoken, rewrite it
