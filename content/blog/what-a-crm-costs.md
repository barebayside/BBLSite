You've heard a CRM would help, but you assume it's expensive enterprise software you can't justify. We hear that worry from almost every owner we talk to — and it's usually backwards. The honest answer: a CRM for a small business is free to start and roughly **$14 to $45 a month** once you're paying, billed annually in USD. The bigger number is what skipping one quietly costs you in forgotten leads and admin time. Let's put both sides on the table.

## What a CRM costs at the low end: nothing

You can run a real CRM for $0 while you learn the habit.

- **HubSpot** has a genuinely free CRM tier covering basic contact and deal tracking.
- **Zoho CRM** has a free plan for a small team with the basics.

These are limited — you'll outgrow them once you want proper automation and reporting — but they're a legitimate place to start. Don't pay for software before you've proven you'll actually use it.

## What a CRM costs once you're paying

Here's the real market, in USD, billed annually. Prices vary by region and billing terms, so treat these as the ballpark, not a fixed global rate.

- **Zoho CRM** — Standard around **$14**/user/mo; Professional around **$23**/user/mo.
- **Pipedrive** — Lite around **$14**/seat/mo, up to Ultimate around **$79**/seat/mo.
- **HubSpot** — Free to start; Starter around **$15–20**/seat/mo; Professional starts around **$100**/seat/mo.
- **Salesforce** — Starter around **$25**/user/mo; Pro around **$100**/user/mo.

For a one-person or small local business, the entry tier of Zoho or Pipedrive — roughly **$14 a month** — covers what you need: contacts, pipeline stages, and follow-up automation.

## When you want more than a CRM

If you also need email marketing, web forms, and analytics, look at a bundle rather than stitching tools together.

**Zoho One** is around **$37**/employee/mo on the all-employee plan, or about **$90**/user on the flexible plan (USD, annual). That gets you the CRM plus marketing automation, forms, analytics, and dozens of other apps in one place. For a small business that would otherwise pay for three or four separate subscriptions, a bundle often works out cheaper overall.

So the realistic spread for a small local business is **$0 to start, $14–45/mo for a solid paid setup**, more only if you deliberately add an enterprise platform.

## The cost that doesn't show up on an invoice

Here's the part most owners miss. The subscription isn't the real cost decision — the cost of *not* having a system is.

Think about where your week actually goes. Sales reps spend only **28% of their time actually selling**; the rest is admin, data entry, meetings, and chasing information (Salesforce State of Sales, 2023). For an owner running everything, that admin tax is even heavier — and it's invisible because no one invoices you for it.

At Bare Bayside Labs, we see this in nearly every business we audit: the leads come in, but nobody's chasing them, and nobody can say which marketing brought them. That's not a software problem. It's the cost of having no system.

## The cost of leads you forget

The most expensive thing about skipping a CRM is the work you already paid to attract and then lost.

Industry research has long suggested that **roughly four in five leads never convert without nurturing** — they go quiet, you get busy, and the follow-up never happens. A CRM's whole job is to stop that: every enquiry gets a record and a status, and follow-ups fire on a schedule whether or not you remember.

We see the turning point hit when manual tracking starts dropping leads — that's the moment the monthly fee becomes the cheapest line in the business.

## Does the maths actually work?

It tends to, even on conservative numbers.

A widely cited figure puts CRM return at **$8.71 for every dollar spent** (Nucleus Research, 2014 — worth flagging the year, and Nucleus's more recent estimate is closer to $3.10 per dollar). Either way, the direction is the same: a system that recovers even one or two jobs a month a busy owner would otherwise have dropped pays for a $14–45 subscription many times over.

The catch is the same one from our [main CRM guide](/pages/blog/do-you-need-a-crm/): the return only shows up if the CRM is actually set up and used. A half-finished CRM is just another bill.

## The real cost is leaving it half-built

This is where the money is won or lost.

A CRM configured once — forms connected, follow-up emails written, pipeline stages defined — pays for itself fast. A CRM that sits half-set-up does nothing but charge you. The subscription is the small number. The setup effort is what determines whether you ever see the return.

If you're weighing a CRM against a spreadsheet on cost grounds specifically, [CRM vs Spreadsheet](/pages/blog/crm-vs-spreadsheet/) draws the line clearly.

## Key Takeaways

- **A CRM is free to start.** HubSpot and Zoho both have genuine free tiers — enough to build the habit before you spend anything.
- **Paid plans are $14–45/mo for a small business**, billed annually in USD, with prices varying by region. The entry tier of Zoho or Pipedrive covers most local businesses.
- **A bundle can be cheaper than separate tools.** Zoho One (around $37/employee/mo) folds CRM, email, forms, and analytics into one subscription.
- **The invisible cost is your time.** Reps spend only 28% of their week selling; for owners the admin tax is heavier still (Salesforce, 2023).
- **The biggest cost is forgotten leads** — work you already paid to attract. Roughly four in five leads never convert without nurturing.
- **The real waste is a half-built CRM.** Set it up properly once and it pays for itself; leave it half-finished and it's just another bill.

## Common Questions

**What's the cheapest way to start with a CRM?**
Free. Both HubSpot and Zoho CRM have free tiers that cover basic contact and deal tracking. Start there, build the habit of logging every lead, and only move to a paid plan once you need automation and reporting — usually within a few months.

**How much should a small business expect to pay?**
For a solid paid setup, roughly $14–45 a month, billed annually in USD, with prices varying by region. The entry tiers of Zoho CRM or Pipedrive sit around $14/user/mo. If you also want email marketing and forms, a bundle like Zoho One (around $37/employee/mo) is often better value than buying tools separately.

**Is a CRM really worth the cost for a small business?**
If you're losing track of leads or can't tell which marketing works, yes. The subscription is small next to the cost of forgotten enquiries and admin time. A frequently cited estimate puts CRM return at $8.71 per dollar spent (Nucleus Research, 2014), though more recent figures are lower — the point is the return dwarfs the fee when the system is used.

**Why do some CRMs cost so much more than others?**
Entry tiers cover the basics. Prices climb steeply once you add advanced automation, large teams, or enterprise features. HubSpot and Salesforce Professional plans start around $100/seat/mo. A small local business rarely needs that — match the plan to what you'll actually use.

**What's the hidden cost people forget?**
Setup. The subscription is the small number; the work to configure forms, write follow-up emails, and define pipeline stages is what determines whether you ever see a return. A half-finished CRM costs you the fee and delivers nothing.
